# tornado-insurance-hazard-repo
## Proposed working project title
Shifting Tornado Hazard in the United States and Emerging Insurance Exposure

## Brief_background
There are two major regions in the United States that are commonly associated with elevated tornado events: the Southeast's region of "Dixie Alley", which includes Arkansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Tennessee and Georgia, and the Great Plains region of "Tornado Alley", which includes Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas and Nebraska. Recent research suggests that the geographic location of tornadoes may be shifting and increasing in frequency in Dixie Alley while decreasing in frequency in Tornado Alley (Gensini and Brooks, 2018)

## Question
This capstone project asks which United States county-year observations are most likely to be classified as high tornado hazard, and whether the elevated tornado hazard is increasing in the Dixie Alley region over time. This project further evaluates whether elevated tornado hazard overlaps with the county-level exposure characteristics that are relevant to insurance risk, including population, housing units and median home value.

## Hypothesis & Predictions
## Hypothesis: 
Tornado hazard is shifting from the traditional Tornado Alley towards Dixie Alley due to an increase in tornado frequency and severity in the Southeast over time.

## Prediction: 
County-year observations within Dixie Alley will most likely be classified as higher tornado hazard compared to Tornado Alley. High tornado hazard county-years will be expected to be characterized by a greater tornado frequency, higher average and maximum tornado intensity, higher counts of strong tornadoes, and greater tornado-related property loss. When combined with the county-level exposure variables including, housing units, population and median home value, the high tornado hazard county-years are expected to represent an elevated tornado-related insurance exposure.
